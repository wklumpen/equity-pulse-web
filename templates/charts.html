Introduction

In the US, Black people, Latinx people , people of color, people with low incomes, people with disabilities, and women have faced discrimination, disenfranchisement, and disinvestment from transportation systems. This marginalization prevents them from reaching the destinations they need. In 2020, the Covid-19 crisis has upended transit operations and threatened how essential workers commute to their jobs.

An equitable transit system recognizes that structural barriers have kept some people from reliable transportation and therefore, from accessing destinations. Reliable, affordable transit service is concentrated specifically to meet the needs of these people — so that everyone has the same access to jobs, healthcare, school, stores, and other opportunities they need to thrive.

This page is divided into five themes, which demonstrate key aspects of transit equity, access to opportunity, and reliable service — and how those outcomes for residents of {{view['title']}} have changed over 2020.

Covid-19 has impacted transit operations and ridership throughout 2020. As a reference, here is a plot of the rolling 7-day average of new cases in {{view['title']}}

Transit agencies have at times limited service during the pandemic in reaction to budgetary or staffing constraints caused by coronavirus, and expanded service to provide more space for social distance. The rate of coronavirus spread also impacts rider behavior, influencing how often people leave their homes to travel for work, run errands, access health care, and access other opportunities and amenities.

We asked: How has job access on public transit changed for each group of people?

This figure shows how the number of jobs that a transit rider can reach within 30 minutes has changed over time. The lines represent the average number of jobs reachable on public transit for people of different racial and ethnic groups and for people using only less expensive transit options (regardless of race or ethnicity). Larger numbers represent better access to jobs. You can also view the most recent data on access to jobs in the Urban Core on a map.

The greatest number of jobs are accessible when riding transit during the morning peak. Accessibility is generally lower at night than during morning peak. This indicates that people who commute during peak hours generally have better access to their jobs than people who commute outside those times. The number of accessible jobs is generally lowest for people who only use less expensive transit options, as they cannot afford certain premium transit routes that grant access around the region.

This figure shows how the average travel time on public transit to the three nearest grocery stores has changed over time. Theme 2: Premium-fare transit systems lead to inequitable access to destinations.

We asked: How many more jobs can people access in 30 minutes if they can afford to pay for premium transit service?

This figure shows changes, over time, in the greater access to jobs on all transit, including more expensive “premium” transit options, compared to the number of jobs that can be reached using only less expensive transit options. The lines represent the average share of additional jobs reachable when using all transit modes for everyone and for low-income people, for whom premium fares are less affordable. You can also view the most recent data on access to jobs via low-cost transit in the MSA on a map.

Premium-fare transit (e.g. commuter rail, express bus) charges higher fares and provides faster service with greater reliability than local routes, which are slower but also cheaper (e.g local bus, subway). Premium-fare transit is unaffordable for some transit riders.

Theme 4: Access to destinations by car outpaces access to destinations by public transit.

We asked: How does access to jobs differ when taking a car instead of riding public transit?

This figure shows changes, over time, in the greater access to jobs reachable by car in 30 minutes as a percentage of jobs reachable by public transit in 30 minutes. You can also view the most recent data on relative access to jobs in the MSA on a map.

We asked: How many more low-income jobs are accessible by car compared to transit?

This figure shows changes, over time, in the additional number of low-wage jobs reachable by car in 30 minutes as a percentage of low-wage jobs reachable by public transit in 30 minutes. The lines represent the average share of additional low-wage jobs reachable by car for everyone and for low-income people, many of whom hold low-wage jobs and for whom having a car is even less affordable. You can also view the most recent data on relative access to low-income jobs in the MSA on a map.

Many low-wage jobs are not located near transit service, so cars provide access to more low-wage jobs than public transit does in almost all circumstances — even though owning a car is too expensive for many workers in low-wage jobs.

Note: Low-income people are defined here as people with household incomes below the poverty line. Low-wage jobs are those paying less than $1,250 per month, or $15,000 per year.
