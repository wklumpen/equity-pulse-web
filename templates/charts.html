Introduction

This page demonstrates the extent to which residents of {{view['title']}} have equitable access to opportunity, convenient travel, and reliable transit service. This page presents the state of transit equity starting in February 2020, and how outcomes for riders have changed alongside changes to the systems and service.

Covid-19 has impacted transit operators and riders alike starting in 2020; for reference, there is a chart of local Covid-19 cases at the bottom of the page.

Transit agencies have at times limited service during the pandemic in reaction to budgetary or staffing constraints caused by coronavirus, and expanded service to provide more space for social distance. The rate of coronavirus spread also impacts rider behavior, influencing how often people leave their homes to travel for work, run errands, access health care, and access other opportunities and amenities.

Transit Service Intensity

Transit agencies regularly adjust schedules, affecting how much transit service people have near home.

How has the daily number of transit trips serving people near their homes changed?

In most regions, transit agencies cut service during the pandemic, with service gradually recovering over time. This was not uniform; many agencies maintained or increased service on routes relied upon by essential workers. But overall, most people have access to fewer transit trips than they did in February 2020.

This chart shows how transit service intensity has changed over time. The lines show how many transit trips are scheduled to make stops, on average each hour, within walking distance of the average resident (disaggregated by different groups). Transit service is a function of density, or how many routes or lines serve an area, and frequency, or how often they run.

Many demographic groups with high levels of transit service and short rides to community destinations like grocery stores also have the least ability to access regional destinations via transit (such as major job centers). This paradox points to how land use segregation and scarce rapid transit service concentrated in affluent areas contribute to inequitable access to regional opportunities. It also underscores the importance of rider-focused measures like access to opportunity: Focusing on equity of transit inputs, such as transit service levels, does not evaluate if riders actually experience equitable transit outcomes.

Fares

How many fewer jobs can people reach in 45 minutes on transit, if they have limited money to spend on fares?

Many regions have a kind of two-tiered transit system, where expensive, often rapid, regional transit is layered on top of a local transit network that is affordable but slow. In these regions, people with low incomes are “priced out” of parts of the transit network, and don’t have as much access as other riders.

This chart shows how access shrinks for people who can’t afford high-cost modes. It compares the number of jobs accessible taking only low-cost transit trips, compared to traveling on the entire transit network, including expensive “premium” service.

A value of 100% indicates that someone taking only low-cost transit trips has the same access to jobs as riders who can pay more for fares. A value of 50% indicates that someone taking only low-cost trips can access half as many jobs as someone with an unlimited budget for fares.

Car Access

The car is a faster mode of travel than public transit, leading many people to choose to buy cars despite the financial costs.

How long are trips to essential destinations, when travelling by transit vs. by car?

Public transit doesn’t need to be faster than the automobile to attract riders. Many people choose transit because it’s more convenient or offers better value. However, few people will choose transit when it is substantially slower than a car trip. And if people are consistently unable to access their needs through the public transit system, they are more likely to buy private vehicles.

Other Definitions

  • Low-Cost Transit: In {{view['title']}}, we defined a low-cost transit trip as a one-way trip with total fares less than ${{view['fare']}}. If the fastest trip to access a job is more than ${{view['fare']}}, then the analysis automatically removes all high-fare, “premium” transit service to see if the job could be accessed in 45 minutes, for less than ${{view['fare']}} taking inexpensive transit modes.

  • Single mothers are female householders with children at home and no spouse or partner present, according to the 2018 five-year U.S. Census American Community Survey.

  • Essential workers are employed in essential industries that operated throughout the Covid-19 pandemic. Transit outcomes for essential workers were estimated based on where workers live, using 2017 Longitudinal Employer-Household Dynamics data.

  • People living in poverty are people whose household incomes are below the federal poverty line, according to the 2018 five-year U.S. Census American Community Survey.

  • Grocery stores are defined as food retailers who belong to the Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP, also known as Food Stamps), according to the USDA.

  • Urgent care facilities provide walk-in emergency medical care, surgery, or recuperative care outside of a hospital or emergency room setting.

  • Colleges or universities include campuses of educational institutions that offer classes and degree programs, from the associate to the doctoral level.

  • Jobs with low wages pay no more than $12,500 in annual wages, according to 2017 U.S. Census Longitudinal Employer-Household Dynamics data.
